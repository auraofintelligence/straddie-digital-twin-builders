Level 2 (L2) living boundary

The island sits inside a bay, and the bay sits inside a watershed.

Level 2 (L2) is where local scene prompts become world simulations: Minjerribah as a living island boundary, Moreton Bay as the receiving water, and the wider catchment as the system that decides what arrives with the tide, rivers, people, freight and care obligations.

Moreton Bay watershed

A civic twin needs water logic, not only roads and buildings.

External source checks place Moreton Bay in a large South East Queensland catchment with major rivers, offshore sand islands, Ramsar wetlands, seagrass, mangroves, intertidal flats and strong cultural, ecological and recreational value. The Commonwealth Ramsar page lists the Moreton Bay Ramsar site as 120,639 hectares, fed from a combined catchment of about 22,000 square kilometres. For this site, that means the first Level 2 (L2) world prompt should start with relationship and responsibility, not ownership.

CatchmentMoreton Bay is fed by multiple river and island systems, so a local simulation needs upstream context.
Sand islandsNorth Stradbroke / Minjerribah is one of the barrier island systems shaping the bay.
WetlandsRamsar and nationally important wetland layers make ecological sensitivity part of the public map.
Public useFerries, recreation, tourism, aquaculture, shipping and local life all share the same receiving waters.
Mirror Country

The Level 2 (L2) twin is a rehearsal room, not a judge with a gavel.

The archive phrase "Mirror Country" is useful because it keeps the spirit right. People can test futures safely: What if this route fails? What if this site becomes public space? What if the ferry pulse changes? What if a heatwave, health surge or festival tests the island? The twin should help people see consequences before they harden into conflict.
